## Executive Summary

The Czech residential rental market is structurally supply-constrained, resulting in vacancy rates well below the EU average. However, significant variation exists across the 13 regional capitals tracked by the calculator. For a **30-year investment horizon**, conservative estimates accounting for economic cycles and city-specific fundamentals are used rather than current (unusually tight) market conditions.

## Methodology

### Why Not Just Use Current Market Conditions?

The Czech rental market in 2025-2026 is exceptionally tight — near the historical low for vacancy. Applying current conditions to a 30-year forecast would be inappropriate because:

1. **Economic cycles**: Czech economy will experience downturns (as in 2008-2009 and 2020)
2. **Tenant turnover time**: Even in tight markets, 2-4 weeks between tenants is normal
3. **Renovation periods**: Long-term owners occasionally renovate (1-2 months)
4. **Seasonal demand**: Demand peaks August-October; lows in December-January
5. **City-specific risks**: Structural depopulation, industrial transition, seasonal markets

### Approach: Three-Level Triangulation

1. **Current market vacancy** — What landlords experience today (2026)
2. **Historical long-term average** — Czech benchmark over multiple cycles
3. **City-specific fundamentals** — Population trends, economic base, demand drivers

**Conservative bias**: Where uncertain, we assume higher vacancy (lower obsazenost) to protect users from overestimating investment returns.

## Part 2: 30-Year Horizon Adjustments

### Conservative Bias Rationale

For a 30-year investment calculator, we do NOT use current (2025-2026) market conditions as defaults:

1. **Current market is historically tight** — below the 5-7% historical vacancy average
2. **Economic cycles**: The Czech Republic will experience 4-6 recessions over 30 years; recessions typically add 2-3% to vacancy rates
3. **Tenant turnover**: Even at 100% demand, turnover creates 2-4 weeks empty per change; at 2-year average tenancy → 2/104 = ~2% annual vacancy minimum
4. **Renovation/maintenance gaps**: Long-term owners occasionally do major work (1-2 months)

### City-Tier Adjustments Applied

| Tier | Current Vacancy | Long-Term Estimate | Adjustment |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|------------|
| Prime metro (Praha, Brno) | 2-4% | 5-6% | +1-3% (conservatism) |
| Strong regional | 4-6% | 7% | +1-3% |
| Average regional | 5-8% | 8-9% | +1-2% |
| Below average | 7-10% | 10% | +1-2% |
| Weak (Ústí) | 10-15% | 13% | Structural anchor |

## Data Limitations & Caveats

### Primary Limitation: No Direct Residential Vacancy Statistics

The Czech Statistical Office (ČSÚ) and Czech National Bank do not publish granular **long-term residential rental vacancy rates by city**. Available data sources are:

- **Commercial property vacancy**: Well-documented (offices: 6.45% Prague Q3 2025), but NOT applicable to residential
- **Census vacancy**: Measures structurally empty units (owner-absent), not rental turnover
- **Short-term rental occupancy** (Airbnb): NOT applicable to long-term residential

The investropa estimates are the most credible directly applicable source, as they explicitly model private residential rental vacancy (not commercial or STR) with triangulation from multiple professional reports.

### Secondary Sources Used for City Ranking

City-specific vacancy adjustments are driven by:
- Deloitte Rent Index (city-level rent trends — proxy for demand-supply balance)
- Dreamville / Expats.cz (qualitative market assessment)
- Population data (structural demand factor)
- CBA Monitor prices (market strength indicator)

These are **proxies, not direct vacancy measurements**. Values should be refreshed when direct residential vacancy survey data becomes available.

## Implementation Notes

### What Changed from Original Default

The original code had a **single hardcoded value of 90%** for all cities:
```typescript
obsazenost: 90,  // 90% default occupancy
```

This was:
- **Too conservative for Praha/Brno**: Market shows 95-97% occupancy
- **Too optimistic for Ústí nad Labem**: Depopulating city likely averages 85-88%
- **Not city-aware**: Switching cities did not update occupancy rate

### Per-City Values Now in calculator-defaults.ts

```typescript
obsazenost defaults by city:
Praha: 95 | Brno: 94 | Plzeň: 93 | Hradec Králové: 93 | Pardubice: 93
Olomouc: 93 | České Budějovice: 92 | Ostrava: 91 | Liberec: 91 | Jihlava: 91
Zlín: 90 | Karlovy Vary: 90 | Ústí nad Labem: 87
```

### Impact on Calculator Output

For Praha (obsazenost 90% → 95%):
- Effective annual rental income increases by ~5.5%
- Example: 17,000 Kč/month × 95% vs 90% = +850 Kč/month effective income
- Over 30 years at compound effect: material improvement in investment case

For Ústí nad Labem (90% → 87%):
- Effective income decreases by ~3.3%
- More accurately reflects structural market risks
